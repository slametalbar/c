Crown corporation

In Canada, Crown corporations (French: Société de la Couronne)

are organizations that are structured like private companies, but are directly and wholly owned by the Canadian federal government or a Canadian provincial government.

Crown corporations are created to advance government policy objectives, and have a long-standing presence in Canada.

Often they provide services to the public that are not economically viable for a private enterprise, or that do not fit exactly within the scope of any ministry.

They represent a form of state-owned enterprise.

Crown corporations are established by an act of parliament or an act of a provincial legislature.

They report to the relevant minister in Cabinet, though they are "shielded from constant government intervention and legislative oversight" and thus "generally enjoy greater freedom from direct political control than government departments."

As of 2022, there were 47 federal Crown corporations in Canada. Provinces operate their own Crown corporations independently of the federal government.





== Structure ==





=== Ownership and accountability ===

In Canada, Crown corporations are directly owned by the government as the enterprise's sole legal shareholder.

(Governments in Canada are sometimes referred to as "the Crown".)

Established by an act of parliament, each Crown corporation is ultimately accountable for the conduct of its affairs to the federal parliament or a provincial legislature through the relevant minister.

Although Crown corporations are owned by the government, they are operated with much greater managerial autonomy than government departments.
